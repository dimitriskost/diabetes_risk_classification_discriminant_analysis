Section 1: Dataset Preparation and Description

This statistical report supports a public-health screening initiative aimed at identifying adult women at high risk for diabetes based on clinical and demographic measurements. The objective of this first section is to introduce the dataset, define the unit of analysis, and justify the application of Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) as an effective method for clinical group separation.

Dataset Overview

The Pima Indians diabetes dataset is a well-known public dataset distributed with the R package MASS. The unit of analysis is an adult female participant in the diabetes screening study. The package contains a training set, Pima.tr, and a test set, Pima.te. The response variable type indicates whether each participant tested positive for diabetes. The explanatory variables include glucose concentration, blood pressure, body mass index, age, and other health-related measurements.

Method Selection and Goals

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) is an ideal method for this scenario because the goal is to classify patients into predefined, clinically meaningful categories. The explanatory variables (predictors) such as glucose concentration, BMI, and blood pressure are continuous, numerical measurements. In the following analysis we will implement the LDA, as is specifically designed to handle continuous predictors and find the best linear combination of these features to maximize the separation between categorical groups.

The group means highlight the clinical differences between the two groups. Women in the “Yes” group show higher average values across all risk factors, such as plasma glucose concentration (‘glu’), body mass index (‘bmi’), and age.

LDA Coefficients for Variable Importance:

Due to differences in measuring scales among our variables and the LDA’s scale sensitivity, the raw coefficients output would not justly measure the importance of each one from its weight. For this reason we will fit the model on the scaled data and extract the variable importance. It is worth noting the LDA model is immutable in scaling, thus we will get the same classification rule and predictions.

# Scaling the training dataset
train_scaled <- train
train_scaled[, -8] <- scale(train_scaled[, -8])

# Fitting the model on scaled data
lda_model_scaled <- lda(type ~ ., data = train_scaled)
standardized_coefs <- abs(lda_model_scaled$scaling[, 1])

Based on the standardized absolute values, glucose dominates the classification rule, having overwhelmingly the heaviest weight. It is followed by pedigree function, age, BMI and number of pregnancies, while blood pressure and skin thickness contribute to a lesser degree.

Plain Language Explanation for Public Health Audience

To identify women at higher risk for diabetes, this model essentially creates a “risk scoring system” based on their medical screening results. It looks at all a patient’s measurements and combines them into a single score. Certain factors carry much more weight in this system, for example high blood sugar (glucose) add significantly more points to a patient’s risk score than their blood pressure does. If a woman’s combined score crosses a specific threshold, the system flags her as showing signs of diabetes, allowing public health professionals to prioritize her for follow-up care and early intervention.

Section 3: Model Fit Evaluation

In this section we will use the fitted model to predict diabetes status for both training and testing datasets and inspect its performance in classifying the women’s records in the two groups accurately. It is important to note again our prior knowledge of the diabetes status for each woman (variable Type), as a means of our comparison. With this information, we can moreover use posterior probabilities to identify observations that are classified with high confidence and observations close to the decision boundary.

# Prediction for the train dataset
pred_train <- predict(lda_model, newdata = train)
train$prediction <- pred_train$class
# Posterior probabilities
train$prob_No <- pred_train$posterior[, 1]
train$prob_Yes <- pred_train$posterior[, 2]

# Prediction fot test dataset 
pred_test <- predict(lda_model, newdata = test)
test$prediction <- pred_test$class
# Posterior probabilities
test$prob_No <- pred_test$posterior[, 1]
test$prob_Yes <- pred_test$posterior[, 2]

Section 4: Visualization of Discriminant Scores and Posterior Probabilities

In this section we visualize the model’s function and performance on the test dataset using 2 plots. They will allow us to assess the linear discriminant classification rule that separates the two groups and discover regions of classification uncertainty.

The above plot displays the distribution of LDA scores for both groups. We notice 2 distinct peaks, indicating a shift between the centers of the two distributions; the women without diabetes (No) concentrating on the negative side, while women with diabetes on the positive. A big area of overlap around 0 is also present, indicating those predictors cannot perfectly separate the two groups, as we found out from our model’s accuracy.

The boxplots above depict how the model’s predicted probability of diabetes aligns with the actual diabetes status of the patients. For the diabetes negative patients, the model shows confidence in it’s predictions, routing from the small size of the box. The median posterior probability is very low (around 10-15%) and the majority of observations cluster near zero. However, several outliers do appear indicating many false-positives; women without diabetes with measurement predictors in the diabetes range. For the diabetes positive group, the model is less confident in it’s predictions and assigns significantly higher probabilities in to the actual diabetic patients (above 60%).

LDA Scores over the predicted Posterior Probabilities form a S-curve. The theoretical decision boundaries (Probability = 0.5 and LD1 = 0) divide the plot into four distinct quadrants, visually mapping our confusion matrix:

  • Top-Right (True Positives): Actual diabetic patients, correctly classified with high probabilities.
  • Bottom-Left (True Negatives): Non-diabetic patients, correctly classified with low probabilities.
  • Top-Left (False Positives): Non-diabetic patients incorrectly flagged as high-risk.
  • Bottom-Right (False Negatives): Diabetic patients missed by the model. The 2-color overlap shows our previously discussed model classification uncertainty.
